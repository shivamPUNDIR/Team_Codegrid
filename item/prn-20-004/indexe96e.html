    National Book Festival Presents, the new series from the Library of Congress showcasing authors, their books, and related Library treasures, will continue with a winter and spring season of events featuring Alice McDermott, Douglas Brinkley, Margaret Atwood, Nan Talese, Richard Ford, Joy Harjo and other authors.

    The season will kick off with \u201cFearless: A Tribute to Irish American Women\u201d on Feb. 6, featuring novelist Alice McDermott in conversation with Pennsylvania Rep. Mary Gay Scanlon and CBS News\u2019 Margaret Brennan.

    The Feb. 6 program begins at 7 p.m. in the Coolidge Auditorium on the ground floor of the Library\u2019s Thomas Jefferson Building, 10 First St. S.E. The event is open to the public and presented in collaboration with the Embassy of Ireland. Free tickets are available at eventbrite.com/e/90177149281. Signed copies of McDermott\u2019s latest novel are available for pre-purchase.

    McDermott\u2019s critically acclaimed eighth novel, \u201cThe Ninth Hour,\u201d set in Irish Brooklyn, was a finalist for the 2017 National Book Critics Circle Award and the 2017 Kirkus Prize for Fiction. Time magazine, The Wall Street Journal, NPR and others named the novel among the top works of fiction in 2017. McDermott is known for her portrayals of Irish American family life.

    Scanlon is an education and human rights advocate representing Pennsylvania\u2019s 5th District. She was first sworn in as a member of Congress on Nov. 13, 2018. Brennan is moderator of CBS News\u2019 \u201cFace the Nation\u201d and the network\u2019s senior foreign affairs correspondent based in Washington. All three women will discuss their Irish backgrounds and family stories.

    National Book Festival Presents author events will be free to the public, though registration will be required.\u00a0Subscribe here\u00a0for email updates on ticketing and book sales. Ticketing links are included for February events here. Ticketing information for future events will be released at\u00a0loc.gov/bookfest/nbf-presents.

    The National Book Festival Presents winter/spring 2020 season will continue with the following events:

    • Thursday, Feb. 13, at 7 p.m., Coolidge Auditorium: \u201cRosa Parks: The History and the Heart\u201d with Parks biographers Douglas Brinkley (\u201cRosa Parks: A Life\u201d) and Jeanne Theoharis (\u201cThe Rebellious Life of Mrs. Rosa Parks\u201d) and NPR\u2019s Michel Martin. The event is in celebration of African American History Month and the Library\u2019s new exhibition \u201cRosa Parks: In Her Own Words,\u201d which will be open for extended hours before the event. Free tickets are required and available at this site: eventbrite.com/e/90351071487. Signed copies of the authors\u2019 books are available for pre-purchase.
    • Thursday, March 12, at 7 p.m., Coolidge Auditorium: \u201cState of the Art: Great American Editors.\u201d Nan Talese, legendary fiction editor, in conversation with novelist Margaret Atwood, author of \u201cThe Handmaid\u2019s Tale.\u201d Moderated by Library of Congress Literary Director Marie Arana. Additional Great American Editors programs are planned to celebrate groundbreaking editors and their authors. Free tickets are required and will be available for reservation on Feb. 13.
    • Friday, March 13, at 9:30 a.m., Coolidge Auditorium: Walter Dean Myers Award for Outstanding Children\u2019s Literature and Symposium, co-sponsored by We Need Diverse Books. Award-winning author Grace Lin will serve as the ceremony\u2019s emcee; the symposium will feature finalists and winners. The event is free and open to the public. No tickets are required.
    • Thursday, March 19, at 7 p.m., Room LJ-119: Author Jeffrey Rosen will discuss his new book, \u201cConversations with RBG: Ruth Bader Ginsburg on Life, Love, Liberty and Law.\u201d Free tickets are required and will be available for reservation on Feb. 13.
    • Thursday, April 2, at 7 p.m., Coolidge Auditorium: Library of Congress Prize for American Fiction: \u201cA Good Story Knows No Borders.\u201d Prize winner Richard Ford will offer a talk and then discuss the universality of fiction and the power of translation with Frank Heibert, Ford\u2019s German translator. Moderated by Library of Congress Literary Director Marie Arana. Free tickets are required and will be available for reservation on March 5.
    • Thursday, April 30, at 7 p.m., Coolidge Auditorium: The closing event of 23rd U.S. Poet Laureate Joy Harjo. Co-sponsored by the Library\u2019s American Folklife Center and Music Division. Free tickets are required and will be available for reservation on March 5.
    • Thursday, May 7, at 7 p.m., Room LJ-119: \u201cDiscovering the Americas We Never Knew.\u201d To celebrate the ancient world, Library of Congress cartography expert John Hessler will discuss his new book about Pre-Columbian cultures, \u201cCollecting for the New World,\u201d published by the Library. Hessler is a Fellow of the Royal Geographical Society in London. Free tickets are required and will be available for reservation on April 2.
    • Thursday, May 21, at 7 p.m., Room LJ-119: Pulitzer Prize-winning cultural critic Philip Kennicott of the Washington Post will discuss his new book, \u201cCounterpoint,\u201d about his life in music. Free tickets are required and will be available for reservation on April 30.

    Book sales for National Book Festival Presents events will be provided in collaboration with East City Books.

    The Library\u2019s National Book Festival, founded in 2001, will continue to host the signature daylong festival this year, celebrating its 20th\u00a0anniversary on Aug. 29, 2020.

    The Library of Congress is the world\u2019s largest library, offering access to the creative record of the United States\u00a0\u2014\u00a0and extensive materials from around the world\u00a0\u2014\u00a0both on-site and online. It is the main research arm of the U.S. Congress and the home of the U.S. Copyright Office.
